# Effective Communication in Teams: Why It's Harder Than We Think

Effective communication in teams is one of the most challenging aspects
of professional collaboration. Not necessarily because communication
itself is inherently complicated, but because people so often talk past
each other. We say one thing but mean another, interpret messages
differently, or assume understanding without verifying it.

In my experience as a leader, I've come to realize that achieving true
mutual understanding between two people is far more difficult than most
of us imagine.

In social settings, communication tends to work reasonably well. Social
norms and expectations guide how we interact. In professional
environments, however, the situation becomes more complex---especially
when people from different disciplines work together.

Consider a simple example: someone from marketing collaborating with
someone from IT. Both may be intelligent and capable professionals, yet
they approach problems with entirely different frameworks, terminology,
and mental models. This can easily lead to misunderstandings. The good
news is that most of these challenges can be solved---once we become
aware of them.

## Step Into the Other Person's World

The first principle of effective communication is empathy: try to
understand the other person's perspective.

Try to see the problem through their experience, their knowledge, and
their constraints.

One situation where I encounter communication challenges frequently is
between product owners and engineers. In my work, I collaborate closely
with product managers while also working alongside developers.

A common scenario looks like this:

A product owner has an idea for a feature or product. They attempt to
explain it, but the concept exists mainly as a mental picture in their
mind. Because it hasn't been fully externalized or clarified, the
explanation often lacks structure.

The developer then takes what they believe they heard and begins
implementing it.

This is one of the worst possible outcomes.

I have seen many cases where, halfway through development, it becomes
clear that the idea wasn't fully understood---or perhaps wasn't fully
formed in the first place. Sometimes the product owner realizes the
concept itself needs revision. Meanwhile, the developer may have spent
one or two weeks building something that ultimately needs to be
discarded.

## Keep Communication Simple

There are many ways to address these challenges, but a few simple
principles make a significant difference.

First, keep your communication simple.

Avoid unnecessary jargon, technical terminology, or industry-specific
language unless it is absolutely required. This applies to everyone
involved, not just one side of the conversation.

Instead, aim for clarity and shared understanding.

## Visualize Your Ideas

One of the most powerful tools for communication is visualization.

If you are explaining a system, a product feature, or a process,
diagrams can be incredibly helpful. A diagram doesn't need to capture
every detail of the final product. Instead, it should illustrate the
thinking process and the sequence of steps involved.

Breaking a concept into clear stages helps align everyone's
understanding.

When a product owner places a diagram in front of a developer, it often
becomes much easier for the developer to ask questions. Visual artifacts
create a shared reference point that both parties can discuss and
refine.

Equally important is dedicating time for clarification. Allow space for
questions, discussion, and feedback. These conversations often resolve
misunderstandings early---before they become expensive problems later.

## Encourage Questions and Verification

Another challenge I frequently observe is that people hesitate to ask
questions. Many fear appearing uninformed or inexperienced.

However, asking questions is not a sign of weakness---it is a sign of
responsibility.

Even asking questions about something you believe you already understand
can be valuable. Sometimes the purpose of the question is not to learn
something new but to verify your interpretation.

This type of verification is extremely important in nearly every
context.

I have seen situations outside of work---such as discussions between
teachers and parents---where two people confidently believed they
understood each other, while in reality they were discussing entirely
different things. Only after someone restated both perspectives did
everyone realize the misunderstanding.

Simply asking, confirming, and repeating key points can dramatically
improve communication.

## Listen More, Speak Less

Another principle I strongly believe in is listening more than speaking.

Be concise. Communicate clearly and precisely. There is no need to
over-explain every detail.

Instead, revisit important points multiple times and express them in
different ways if necessary. This can reinforce understanding without
overwhelming the listener.

If I sense that someone may not be asking enough questions, I sometimes
ask them to explain the concept back to me. While this can initially
feel slightly uncomfortable, the goal is not to test them---it is to
ensure that we are discussing the same thing.

Over time, as people become more familiar with each other's
communication styles and working methods, these interactions become
easier and more natural.

## Communicating With Teams

Communication becomes even more complex in group settings.

When communicating with a team, you are no longer dealing with a
one-to-one interaction but rather one-to-many.

In these situations, visual aids become even more important. I have
often used dozens of small notes, sketches, and diagrams to explain a
concept. Sometimes people even take these notes with them afterward to
remember the discussion.

This highlights an important truth: people rarely remember long verbal
explanations. The more we say, the easier it becomes for listeners to
misunderstand the core message.

Clarity often comes from saying less.

## Present the Problem Before the Solution

When facilitating team discussions---such as planning meetings---I
typically start by presenting the problem rather than the solution.

Even if I have already spent considerable time thinking about potential
solutions, I prefer to let the team explore ideas first. I might
introduce the challenge and then allow ten minutes for discussion.

What could the solution be?

This approach has several advantages.

First, it creates shared ownership of the outcome. The solution becomes
the team's solution rather than the leader's directive.

Second, it encourages diverse perspectives. People often propose ideas
that are significantly better than my initial assumptions.

Because I have already thought about the problem beforehand, I can guide
the conversation by asking questions and highlighting potential
pitfalls---without dominating the discussion.

## Communication Builds Motivation

There is another important side effect of this approach: motivation.

When people contribute ideas and participate in shaping the solution,
they feel more invested in the outcome. Recognizing and validating their
contributions strengthens trust and confidence.

Even if the final solution aligns closely with what I initially
envisioned, the process of collaborative discovery creates stronger
engagement and ownership within the team.

## The Core Principles of Effective Communication

If I were to summarize effective communication in a few key principles,
they would be:

-   Keep communication simple\
-   Use visualization whenever possible\
-   Verify understanding through questions and feedback\
-   Listen more than you speak\
-   Encourage collaboration and shared ownership of solutions

Effective communication remains one of the most difficult skills to
master---not because the principles are complex, but because people are
incredibly different. Each of us interprets information through our own
experiences, assumptions, and perspectives.

Recognizing this complexity is the first step toward communicating
better.
